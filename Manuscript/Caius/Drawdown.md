Day 182,625.

Anaya came to the apartment today. In person. She spent sixteen hours of her loop on flights and a taxi to stand in my kitchen and say one sentence, which is how I knew the sentence before she said it. Good news survives a phone call.

"The program is over, Caius. It has been over for thirty years. I am the last one still pretending otherwise, and you are the reason I was pretending, and I'm done."

I made her coffee. She watched me do it and said nothing, which from Anaya is a eulogy.

The accounting, because the accounting is what I am for:

Three hundred and four years. One hundred eleven thousand iterations, less the mornings lost to weather, grief, and the four sabbaticals she made me take. Every one of those mornings, somewhere, a crew rebuilt the beamline from memorized procedure — eidetics chanting torque specifications like psalms — so that we could put more energy into one point of spacetime than this universe was ever designed to notice. Energy spikes. Standing waves. The ionosphere series, which I will not dignify with figures. Total return across all programs: zero. Not small. Not ambiguous. Zero, with error bars too narrow to hide a hope in.

A position is not wrong because it loses. A position is wrong when the thesis breaks and you hold anyway. So the honest entry, the one I will make myself write: the thesis broke decades ago, and I held. I held because the size of what I'd already paid felt like evidence. There is a name for that error. I have fired men for that error.

She told me what finally convinced her. Not our data — hers was always cleaner than her hopes. A retired chemistry teacher in Leipzig. A bench, a beam splitter, and a hundred and forty-six thousand mornings of the same polarization measurement, every result identical, a number so far past impossible that the word stops working. One amateur with nothing but patience measured what three centuries of my infrastructure could not buy.

I called that dataset unserious once. I remember saying it — which is the particular cruelty of this mind in this world: I will remember saying it at the same temperature, forever, while the woman I said it to forgets nothing either and was kind enough today not to quote me.

Anaya asked what I do now. I told her I would reconcile the books and get back to her. She laughed, and I checked, and it had been eleven years.

So. The reconciliation, written down where writing actually keeps — in the hand, in the act, in me:

The model was wrong about physics for three hundred and four years.

The model has never once been wrong about people.

The amateurs did not beat us with better instruments. They beat us because they were many, and patient, and free, and there is no asset on this dead repeating earth so badly mispriced as a human being with nothing left but time. I have spent three centuries trying to crack the lock. The lock does not want cracking. But the people who will — they will need rooms, and ledgers, and someone who knows what everyone is worth and what everyone is owed and who never, ever forgets.

That is a position I can hold.

The man at the top of this page wanted to chase it down properly. He never got his morning. I have had one hundred eleven thousand of them, and I have spent them all on the wrong trade, and tonight, for the first time in three hundred years, I find I agree with him completely.

Tomorrow, first thing.

Position closed.
