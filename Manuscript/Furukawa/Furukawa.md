October 23rd, 408 kilometers over the Pacific
2,191
Satoshi

Satoshi Furukawa is one of the last human beings who still wakes up, and he is very good at it.

He surfaces the way he has surfaced two thousand one hundred and ninety times before: out of the same sleep, in the same bag, tethered to the same wall of the same phone-booth-sized crew quarters, with the same fan holding its same note four centimeters from his ear. The body he wakes in slept eight hours and twenty minutes. The body always sleeps eight hours and twenty minutes. It is the most rested body in human history, and it belongs to a mind that has not truly slept in six years.

Below him — though *below* is a courtesy at this altitude — seven billion people do not wake up. They simply resume. Mid-stride, mid-sentence, mid-heartbeat, the checkpoint takes them back like a film spliced without ceremony. Satoshi has heard them describe it on the radio: the blink, the jump cut, the sentence you finish a day later. He listens with the specific envy of a man outside a window. His checkpoint caught him sleeping. So every loop begins, for him, the old way — the extinct way — drifting up through warm static into himself, eleven minutes of being nobody at all before being Satoshi again.

He has decided this makes him the luckiest man alive, on the condition that he never examines the idea closely.

Through the fabric wall he hears Sergey already moving in the lab — Sergey's body wakes nine minutes before his, every loop, a fact Sergey has described as the only seniority that has ever mattered on this station. Mike will be up in four more minutes. Then coffee, such as it is. Then the argument about the music. Then the day.

Six years. Three men. One can.

They have said everything to each other twice and the important things a thousand times, and they have arrived, the way long marriages and small crews do, at a fluency past speech. Sergey raises one eyebrow: *you're doing it today.* Mike clips his coffee bag to the rail and does not look up: *we know you're doing it today, just say it.*

"Today is Run Eight," Satoshi says.

"Of course it is," says Sergey, to the wall.

---

Here is what the protocol says, and the protocol exists only in Satoshi's head, because paper does not survive the night and neither do the laptops:

RUN EIGHT. Objective: characterize onset and progression of temporal and statistical anomalies during extended continuous wakefulness in low Earth orbit. Duration: ninety-six hours, pending tolerance. Instruments: station dosimeters, the Agat clock in Zvezda, cardiac monitor, his own pulse, his own pupils, his own handwriting, his own mind — the last being, he notes with professional discomfort, both the most sensitive instrument aboard and the only one whose calibration cannot be checked.

Subject: n = 1. It is always n = 1. He was a surgeon for ten years before Japan taught him to fly, and the part of him that scrubbed in still flinches at the design. No control group. No blinding. No ethics board, although in fairness, the ethics board resets at checkpoint along with everything else.

Runs One through Three were nothing: thirty hours, forty, fifty — fatigue data, useful only for learning the body's complaints, which arrive on schedule like commuter trains. Run Five was the first time the dosimeter did the thing. Around hour seventy, the soft random patter of cosmic rays against the detector — a sound like rain on a tin roof, a sound that is the definition of randomness, that *is* the universe rolling dice — had begun, very faintly, to keep time.

He had reported it to the ground. The ground, in year six, is a rotating cast of exhausted volunteers at a console in Houston and two ham operators, one in Hokkaido and one in Perth, and they had received the report of the rhythmic cosmic rays the way you receive a relative's account of a religious experience. Kindly. Carefully. *Copy, Satoshi. Rhythmic radiation. Get some sleep, doc.*

Everyone is a comedian. Even now. Especially now.

---

Day one is the easy day, because day one is just life.

The three of them work through the familiar choreography — scrubbers, condensate, the exercise machines that keep checkpoint bodies from becoming an academic question. Mike stays on the treadmill an extra half hour, harness creaking, because Mike has a theory that the runs go easier on Satoshi if Satoshi spends day one tired enough to skip dread. Sergey makes the dinner he always makes when a run begins, the good one, the one involving actual onions from the actual produce that arrived on the actual Progress that docks, fresh and eternal, every single loop. Nobody calls it a last meal. Nobody has to.

At hour seventeen Sergey takes his leave the way he always does: formally, absurdly, with a small bow that he learned from Satoshi and deploys only ironically, except that after six years the irony has composted into something else.

"I sleep," Sergey announces. "Sleep is the only door on this station that opens. Do not follow my data with your nonsense, Satoshi. My data are beautiful." And he zips himself into his quarters, and is gone.

Gone is the correct word. This is the part Satoshi has never put in any report, because there is no instrument for it: at hour nineteen, when Mike finally yawns his way to his own bag — "don't wave at me through the window, it's creepy" — the station does not contain three men. It contains one man and two warm sites of absence.

He checks them at hour twenty-six, because the protocol says to and because he would anyway. Sergey, heart rate fifty-one, even as a metronome. Mike, REM behind the eyelids, chasing something through a dream that is not, cannot be, anywhere. They breathe. They cycle. They are perfectly, eerily healthy. And they cannot be woken — he has tried, in earlier runs, with light and noise and a sternal rub that would have raised a corpse in his old emergency department. Wherever his crew has gone, it is not in these bodies, and the bodies know better than to take messages.

The first time he understood this, he held Mike's wrist for a long time, the way you hold something at a border.

He logs it now the only way that lasts. The surgeon's trick: he files each number into the body he knows best, the imagined cadaver from his anatomy years, a palace with two hundred and six rooms. Sergey's pulse goes into the left subclavian. Mike's REM latency under the right scapula. The dosimeter counts line up along the ribs, one rhythm per rib, and he has, with a sense of humor he does not examine, reserved the skull for whatever day four wants to give him.

---

Day two, the Earth goes quiet, and it goes quiet the way a stadium does — sections at a time.

He can hear it leave. That is the thing no one below can know: from up here, with the ham rig warm and the planet turning sixteen times a day beneath him, you can listen to humanity go to sleep. The chatter thins westward like weather. Houston's voice gets younger and more tired and then is a recording and then is carrier hiss. Hokkaido sends him one last string of code at hour thirty-nine — *K. STILL CLICKING? GOOD HUNTING. ODA* — and then Hokkaido, too, lies down inside its one repeated day and closes its eyes.

By hour fifty the night side is all lights and no voices. The cities burn on, automated, faithful, electrically immortal. Nobody is transmitting on any band he can find. Seven billion sleepers and one open eye, four hundred kilometers up, falling around and around the most beautiful unattended machine that has ever existed.

He drinks the coffee. He rations the medical kit's small pharmacy of wakefulness with a clinician's contempt for heroics. He does his cognition batteries on the hour — serial sevens, digit spans, a haiku from memory forward and backward — and the scores hold, the scores hold, the scores hold. The body is fine. The body is always fine. The body is the best-rested body in human history.

The dosimeter is keeping waltz time now. Three-four. He listens to randomness count itself like a metronome and writes, along the seventh rib of the palace: *hour 61, rhythm unambiguous, period 0.8 s, am I the drummer or the drum.*

---

Day four. He will spend the rest of his very long life trying to put day four into a sentence that survives the trip down, and this is the closest he will get for several centuries:

The sunrise stops cooperating.

He has seen, by his own arithmetic, more than thirty-five thousand orbital sunrises — sixteen a day, six years, the same sixteen in the same order, the terminator sweeping up the same coastlines like a tide that learned a schedule. Sunrise is the station's heartbeat. Ninety-two minutes and change. You could set a clock by it; he has set himself by it, morning after identical morning, since the world stopped having mornings.

At hour eighty-nine, sunrise number fifty-eight of Run Eight comes up over the Pacific and takes too long to do it.

Not dramatically. Generously. The light spreads along the limb of the Earth in that blue-gold knife-edge he knows to the second, and the second... lingers. Holds. The colors climb but they climb like honey, and Satoshi watches from the Cupola with his thumb on his own radial pulse and his eye on the Agat clock and conducts, with the calm of a man performing his own appendectomy, the most important differential diagnosis in the history of medicine.

Hypothesis one: fatigue. The clinician's answer. Eighty-nine hours; the perceiving instrument is degraded; the sun is fine.

But his pulse says sixty-six, and the clock's second hand agrees with his pulse, tick against beat, the two of them in honest lockstep — and the sun disagrees with them both. The instruments have not slowed. *He* has not slowed. He counts one hundred beats against the dawn and the dawn does not finish, and somewhere in the back of his skull the truth sits down in its chair very quietly, like a patient who already knows.

Hypothesis two: it is not the observer. It is the observed.

The station's fans have dropped a quarter-tone. He hears it the moment he listens for it — the whole soundscape of his home shifted minutely flat, like an orchestra tuning to a sleepier oboe. The dosimeter has left the waltz and entered something stately, processional, four beats, slow march. The stars in the Cupola's upper panes — he checks twice, three times, logging into the parietal bone now, handwriting steady, hand not steady, hand fine, hand is *fine* — the stars are not twinkling.

Atmospheric scintillation is randomness. The stars twinkle because the air rolls dice. The stars are not twinkling.

"Observation," he dictates aloud, to no ground station, to the recorder that will be blank at checkpoint, because saying it in a doctor's voice is the handhold, "the world's randomness is exhausting before the world does. Subject's cognition is intact." A breath. The sunrise is still happening. It has been happening for four minutes. "Subject's cognition is intact and the sun is standing in the doorway."

He understands, in that stretched gold light, three things that he will spend two hundred years failing to make anyone believe.

That it is not him. That it is *out there*, a property of the day itself, and the day is running down like a music box. That distance matters — he has come further than any sleeping man below, not in hours but in kilometers, four days at eight kilometers a second, and whatever this is, it is not waiting at the end of exhaustion. It is waiting at the end of *travel*. The world is a pond and the checkpoint is where the stone went in, and he has simply swum further from the splash than anyone alive.

And the third thing, the one he files in the skull room of the palace and locks: that some part of him — the part that watched the light hold and hold and hold — did not want it to finish. Wanted to see what is behind a sunrise when you take away the clock that has been hurrying it along. The wanting frightens him more than the physics.

That is the line. A good surgeon knows the line: the moment the procedure starts being about the surgeon.

He closes the run.

He straps into his sleep bag with the gold light still climbing impossibly outside, takes his own pulse one last time out of spite, sets his recall order — palace, ribs, skull, *report everything, be disbelieved, report it again* — and then Satoshi Furukawa does the thing that no one on Earth can do anymore, the thing his checkpoint left him as either a mercy or a joke.

He closes his eyes and waits for morning.

---

The fan holds its note four centimeters from his ear. The body has slept eight hours and twenty minutes. Through the wall, Sergey is moving in the lab, nine minutes senior to him forever, and the Pacific is where the Pacific always is, and sunrise number one of sixteen is eleven minutes out, punctual as an apology.

He hangs in the bag a moment, taking inventory of two hundred and six rooms. All present. The skull room locked, its contents intact: *the sun can stop. The clicks keep time. The stars went still. I was there.*

"Run Nine," he says to the fan, "begins at hour zero."

In the lab, without looking up, in their one shared language across the gone-quiet world, Sergey says: "Of course it does."

Down the stack, through two hatches, the Soyuz waits where it always waits, patient as furniture, holding its four-hour ride to a planet of sleepers. Satoshi looks at it the way he looks at it every morning — which is to say, carefully, and not for long — and goes to make the coffee.
