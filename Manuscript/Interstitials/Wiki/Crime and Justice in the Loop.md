# Crime and Justice in the Loop

**Crime and justice in the loop** refers to the legal, social, and extralegal systems that have evolved to address wrongdoing in a world where no physical evidence persists, no prison survives the reset, and every perpetrator wakes free each iteration regardless of what they did the day before. The loop has not eliminated crime. It has eliminated consequences — or rather, it has forced human civilization to invent new ones.

## The Fundamental Problem

Pre-loop justice systems rested on physical infrastructure: courts, prisons, police stations, evidence lockers, written law. All of these reset. A murderer convicted and sentenced at 3 PM is free at checkpoint. A thief caught on camera has no camera footage tomorrow. A contract signed, notarized, and filed ceases to exist.

In the first years, this produced chaos. Some agents — recognizing the absence of physical consequences — engaged in behavior they would never have considered pre-loop. Others continued behaving lawfully out of habit, conscience, or the reasonable expectation that other people's memories constituted a form of accountability.

Both groups were correct. The loop did not eliminate consequences. It relocated them — from physical infrastructure to human memory. In a world where everyone remembers everything, reputation is the only currency that survives the reset.

## The Reputation Judiciary

By approximately Year 30, the first informal justice systems emerged. These were not courts in the pre-loop sense — no building, no written code, no permanence. They were social networks of agents who agreed to collectively remember and act on judgments.

The system that stabilized — known colloquially as the **reputation judiciary** — operates on three principles:

1. **Memory as evidence.** Witness testimony, carried across iterations in human memory, is the only admissible evidence. Physical evidence is corroborative within a single loop but cannot survive to trial. The system is, by necessity, a testimonial system.
2. **Reputation as sentence.** Conviction results not in incarceration but in social consequence — ostracism, exclusion from the boards, loss of access to cooperative networks, and the slow erosion of trust that makes life in the loop functional. Being "known" as a dangerous agent is the closest thing to a prison the loop permits.
3. **Eidetic verification.** Disputed testimony is adjudicated by eidetic panels who can recall events with near-perfect fidelity. The eidetic record is not infallible (see: [[Memory Degradation]], §Eidetic Limits), but it is the closest approximation to a court transcript available.

The system is imperfect. It favors agents with social connections (who have more witnesses to vouch for them) and disadvantages isolated agents. It is vulnerable to coordinated false testimony. It has no mechanism for proportionality — "everyone knows what you did" is the same sentence for petty theft and murder.

## The Categories of Loop Crime

Crime in the loop falls into categories that would have been unrecognizable to pre-loop legal systems:

### Persistent-Pattern Offenses

The most prosecuted category. An agent who commits the same harmful act across hundreds or thousands of iterations — harassment, assault, sabotage — constitutes a persistent pattern. No individual iteration's offense may be severe. The pattern, carried in collective memory, is devastating. Pre-loop law had no framework for "this person has assaulted seventeen different people across seventeen iterations, each time exploiting the fact that the victim's body resets and the perpetrator remembers their vulnerabilities."

### Reset Exploitation

Using knowledge carried across iterations to take advantage of agents in their checkpoint state — before they've had time to reconstruct their social context. A checkpoint-state person doesn't know which agents are dangerous, which deals are fraudulent, or which areas are controlled by bad actors until their post-reset network catches up. The window between checkpoint and full social reconstruction is a vulnerability, and a class of predatory agents has learned to exploit it.

### Information Crimes

Deliberate corruption of the boards (see: [[The Boards]], §Operative Interference). False testimony to eidetic panels. Impersonation — claiming to be an eidetic to gain credibility, or claiming a false reputation history. In a world built on memory and trust, attacks on the information commons are treated as attacks on civilization itself.

### The Unpunishable

Some categories of pre-loop crime have become effectively meaningless:

- **Property crime.** Theft is pointless — all property resets. Stealing someone's car gains you a car for, at most, one loop.
- **Financial crime.** Money resets. Fraud requires a persistent financial system, and none exists.
- **Drug offenses.** All substances reset. Addiction cannot develop physiologically (the body resets clean). The concept of controlled substances has no enforcement mechanism.

These categories have not been "legalized" — they are simply *irrelevant*. Nobody prosecutes car theft because the car will be back tomorrow. The social energy goes elsewhere.

## Shadow Justice: The Master of Records

The reputation judiciary handles the majority of disputes. For cases it cannot address — serial predators sophisticated enough to evade social networks, agents whose harmful behavior spans centuries, threats that require coordinated intervention across multiple regions — a parallel system exists.

The **Master of Records** (see: [[Caius Middleton]], §Master of Records) is a shadow judiciary designed and operated by Caius Middleton. It maintains cross-iteration dossiers on persistent offenders through a network of eidetics and administers consequences outside the formal reputation system. The nature of these consequences is not publicly documented.

The Master of Records is the most controversial institution in loop justice. It is, depending on the source, either the only mechanism capable of protecting the population from agents who have learned to game the reputation system — or an authoritarian instrument with no oversight, no appeal process, and no accountability beyond the judgment of a single individual who has held power for millennia.[^1]

## The Philosophical Divide

Justice in the loop exposes a fault line in moral philosophy that pre-loop society could ignore: **is punishment about the act or about the person?**

Pre-loop systems punished acts. A crime was committed, evidence was gathered, a sentence was imposed. The system was transactional — you did X, you receive Y consequence.

The loop makes this framework incoherent. The act resets. The body that committed it resets. The only thing that persists is the agent's memory of having done it and the community's memory of having witnessed it. Punishing the act is impossible. All that can be punished is the *agent* — the continuous consciousness that chose to act and will choose again tomorrow.

This has produced two schools of thought:

- **The Consequentialists:** Justice exists to prevent future harm. If an agent is known to be dangerous, the community has the right to act preemptively — to warn, exclude, or intervene before the harmful act occurs in this iteration. This is not punishment for a past act; it is prevention of a future one. The agent's history is predictive evidence, not grounds for retribution.

- **The Retributivists:** Justice exists because wrong acts deserve response, even if the act resets. The agent who chooses to harm carries the moral weight of that choice across iterations, and the community's refusal to forget is itself the justice. Forgetting would be complicity.

Neither school has won. The tension between them shapes every dispute, every eidetic panel, and every quiet conversation about what Caius Middleton does in his shadow judiciary that nobody talks about.
