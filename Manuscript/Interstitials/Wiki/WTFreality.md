# WTFreality

**WTFreality** is the world's longest-running prediction market, rebuilt from memory each iteration from a state maintained by a dedicated scribe collective (see: [[The Boards]], §Scribes). It tracks public consensus on fundamental questions about the nature of the loop through continuously-updated betting odds. WTFreality does not use money — which resets — but reputation credit, an informal social currency backed by track record.

Founded in approximately Year 80 as an informal board thread titled "wanna bet?", WTFreality has grown into one of the most-cited institutions in loop culture. Its odds are referenced in academic disputes, political negotiations, and casual conversation. "What's it trading at?" is a standard way of asking what the collective believes about any given theory.

## How It Works

WTFreality maintains a set of standing markets, each structured as a question with a range of possible outcomes. Any agent can take a position. Positions are tracked by the WTFreality scribes, who memorize the current state each iteration and reconstruct it on the boards each morning.

**There is no money.** The loop resets all physical currency, and no one has successfully created a persistent currency accepted widely enough to matter. WTFreality runs on reputation credit — a ledger of correct and incorrect calls maintained by the scribe collective. An agent's WTF Score (formally: Predictive Track Record, informally: "luck") is the ratio of correct calls to total positions, weighted by confidence level and adjusted for the base probability of the outcome.[^1]

Top-ranked bettors — agents with centuries-long streaks of correct calls — are known as **Oracles**. Being an Oracle grants no formal authority, but it grants something more practical in loop culture: people listen to you. An Oracle who moves a position can shift the odds. An Oracle who opens a new market creates a question that the culture starts tracking. The power is soft, but it is real, and Oracle status has been the subject of manipulation, fraud, and at least one known assassination (see: the Reykjavik Incident, Year ~2,800, disputed).

## Standing Markets

The canonical standing markets, as of the current iterative consensus:

### 1. Will the Loop Break Today?

The oldest market. Binary outcome: yes or no. Opened on Day 1 of WTFreality's existence. The "no" position has paid out on every single iteration for approximately 10,000 years. The "yes" position has never paid out.

Despite this, the "yes" position never drops to zero. A small but consistent population of agents bets "yes" every iteration — some out of genuine conviction, some as a daily ritual, some because the expected-value calculation on a world-ending event is philosophically incoherent and they prefer to have been right if it happens.

**Current odds:** 500,000:1 against ("yes" pays 500,000 reputation credits per 1 wagered). These odds have been stable for approximately 3,000 years.

### 2. What Will Cause the Loop to Break?

A multi-outcome market tracking the leading causal theories. Odds shift in response to scientific discoveries, public events, and scribe drift. The major categories, with approximate current odds:

| Theory | Odds | Movement |
|---|---|---|
| RNG-related event (exhaustion, exploitation, cascade failure) | 8:1 | ↓ from 25:1 post-Leipzig |
| External intervention (simulation operators act) | 15:1 | Stable |
| Voyager correction (see: [[Bug 1 Theories]], §Syrah's Voyager Theory) | 12:1 | ↑ from 9:1 (lost ground post-Leipzig) |
| Cumulative entropy death (the loop naturally expires) | 20:1 | Stable |
| Agent action — deliberate (someone does the right thing) | 10:1 | Volatile |
| Agent action — accidental (someone stumbles into it) | 30:1 | Stable |
| Divine intervention / metaphysical cause | 45:1 | ↑ from 35:1 (slowly losing adherents) |
| The loop is already broken and we haven't noticed | 100:1 | Novelty position, popular with contrarians |
| Jeff's driving somehow | 200:1 | Joke position. Has occupied this slot since Year ~400. Nobody remembers who Jeff is.[^2] |
| Caius does something (unspecified) | 6:1 | Perpetual favorite. Moves on every Caius-related rumor. |
| Something nobody has thought of yet | 3:1 | The only position that has shortened consistently over time |

The "Something nobody has thought of yet" category is the market's most interesting feature. It has been the shortest odds for every era and has never paid out. Its persistent popularity reflects a deep structural pessimism in loop culture — a collective admission that the smartest thing the market has ever done is bet on its own ignorance.

### 3. Will We Remember?

The existential market. If the loop breaks, do the agents retain their accumulated memories, or do they revert to their Year 0 selves?

| Outcome | Odds |
|---|---|
| Yes — full memory retention | 3:1 |
| No — complete memory wipe | 2:1 |
| Partial — some remember, some don't | 4:1 |
| "Depends on what 'we' means" | 5:1 |

The "depends" position was added in approximately Year 1,200 after a philosophical movement argued that 10,000 years of memory in a pre-loop body constitutes a different entity, and that the question of "remembering" is incoherent if the person doing the remembering is not the person who accumulated the memories. This position has a small but passionate following.

### 4. Side Markets

WTFreality also hosts rotating side markets on more specific questions. These change frequently and include:

- **Which hour (UTC) will the loop break?** A 24-slot market. The midnight slot trades at extreme odds because if the loop breaks at midnight, nobody would know — it would look identical to a normal reset.
- **Will the next major discovery come from physics, biology, or philosophy?** Physics has dominated since Leipzig.
- **Is the wiki article about WTFreality more or less accurate than WTFreality itself?** A meta-market that trades on the accuracy of this document. Currently at 1:1. Nobody is confident in either direction.

## Cultural Significance

WTFreality occupies a unique position in loop culture as simultaneously a serious analytical instrument and a cultural joke. The odds are cited in academic papers. The odds are also the subject of memes, drinking games, and at least one religion (the Church of Even Odds, Year ~3,400, now disbanded).

The market's most important function may be its least dramatic: it provides a shared numerical language for collective belief. Before WTFreality, debates about the loop's cause were qualitative and unresolvable. After WTFreality, the culture could say "the Voyager theory is trading at 12:1 and falling" — not as a claim about truth, but as a measurement of where the conversation stands. The market does not track what is true. It tracks what people think is true. The distinction, in a world without persistent records, is the only distinction available.

## Accuracy

WTFreality's track record on minor markets (side bets, hourly predictions, factual disputes) is well-documented and respectable — roughly 68% calibration on questions with deterministic outcomes.

Its track record on the core question — will the loop break and what will cause it — is, by definition, untestable. The market has predicted "no" for 10,000 years and been correct every time. When the loop finally does break, the market will either be right (if the cause matches the leading theory) or spectacularly wrong (if it doesn't). Either way, there will be nobody to collect.

The post-timequake irony, for those in a position to appreciate it: the market had "RNG-related event" at 8:1. Not bad. But nobody had "the RNG team intentionally triggers global vitrification as a live test and everything goes sideways." WTFreality was right about the category and wrong about everything else. The market's epitaph, if epitaphs were still being written, would be: *close enough to be frustrating.*

## Notes

[^1]: The exact formula for the WTF Score has been revised nineteen times and is maintained by a committee of three scribes who refuse to publish it, arguing that publishing the formula would allow strategic manipulation. Critics argue that not publishing the formula *already* allows strategic manipulation, just by fewer people. This dispute is ongoing.

[^2]: The leading theory is that Jeff was a specific person in the first century who was known for catastrophically bad driving. The position was added as a joke, persisted because it was funny, and has outlived all memory of its origin by approximately 9,600 years. It is now cultural infrastructure. Multiple attempts to remove or replace it have failed. Jeff's driving is the loop's most durable meme.
