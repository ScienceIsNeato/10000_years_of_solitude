# Pets in the Loop

**Pets** — domesticated animals kept for companionship — occupy a unique and emotionally fraught position in loop society. Animals are governed entirely by the physics engine. They have no agent-engine component. They do not retain memories across iterations. Every loop, every pet in the world wakes up in its checkpoint state with no knowledge of any previous iteration, including the thousands of iterations in which its owner loved it.

Your dog does not know you. Has never known you. Will never know you across a reset.

## The First-Meeting Problem

The emotional core of pet ownership in the loop is the daily re-encounter. Each iteration, your pet meets you for what is, from its perspective, the first time. The owner carries thousands of years of relationship. The animal carries nothing.

For dog owners, this manifests as the daily re-earning of trust. A dog whose checkpoint state is "comfortable with its owner" will behave accordingly — the body remembers the relationship, because the body is reset to a state in which the relationship existed. But a dog whose checkpoint state is "recently adopted" or "nervous around strangers" must be won over again every loop. Owners of anxious dogs have developed elaborate first-contact rituals refined over millennia: the exact tone of voice, the precise approach angle, the specific treat that produces the fastest trust response. Some of these protocols are documented on the boards; most are private, intimate, and fiercely protected.

Cat owners report that the experience is largely unchanged.

## The Agent-Engine Question

Whether animals possess any agent-engine component is one of the oldest unresolved questions in loop science. The prevailing consensus is no — animals reset completely, behavior and all, and exhibit no evidence of cross-iteration memory or personality development.

The dissenting view, maintained by a persistent minority, holds that certain animals exhibit behavioral variations across iterations that cannot be explained by physics-engine determinism alone. Proponents cite cases of dogs that appear to recognize loop-veteran owners "too quickly," birds that deviate from deterministic flight patterns, and a famous (and disputed) series of observations involving a pod of dolphins off the coast of New Zealand that appeared to respond to a researcher's whistle in a manner inconsistent with their checkpoint behavioral state.

The experimental evidence is inconclusive. The ethical implications of designing definitive experiments — which would require subjecting animals to controlled consciousness-loss events to test for memory persistence — have prevented the question from being resolved. The debate continues on the boards, where it generates more heat per iteration than almost any other topic in natural philosophy.[^1]

## Practical Considerations

- **Veterinary care.** All animal injuries and illnesses reset. A pet diagnosed with cancer in one iteration is healthy in the next. Veterinary medicine, as a field concerned with long-term animal health, has largely been repurposed toward behavioral science and the agent-engine question. Emergency veterinary care still exists for within-loop injuries.
- **Training.** No training persists across iterations. A dog trained over months to sit, stay, and roll over knows none of these things the next loop. Owners who wish their pets to perform trained behaviors must retrain each iteration. The speed of retraining has been studied extensively — a dog that "learned" to sit on Day 1 of its first iteration learns no faster on Day 1 of its ten-thousandth iteration. This is considered strong evidence against an animal agent engine.
- **Reproduction.** Animals reproduce within a single loop if the pregnancy duration fits within the mother's consciousness span. This is a non-issue for most species — a mouse can be born, grow, reproduce, and die within a single human loop. Larger animals with long gestation periods — elephants, whales — have not produced a single new offspring in 10,000 years. Every elephant alive is the same elephant, in the same body, in the same place, at the same gestational stage, as October 23, 2011.
- **Wildlife.** Wild animals behave identically in the early hours of each iteration — same bird on same branch, same squirrel on same transformer (see: [[The Boards]], §The Florida Squirrel). Agent perturbations cause divergence as the loop progresses. By hour 6, the butterfly effect of billions of humans making different choices has propagated through ecosystems. By hour 12, wildlife behavior is essentially unpredictable. This transition — from deterministic to chaotic — mirrors the RNG degradation pattern at a macro scale and was among the earliest observational clues that something about the loop's physics was not random.[^2]

## Emotional Impact

The psychological literature on pet ownership in the loop is extensive and largely devastating. The relationship is asymmetric in a way that no pre-loop human bond was: one party carries the entire history; the other party is new every day.

Some agents find this beautiful — the perpetual innocence, the daily gift of being loved without expectation or memory of past failures. A faction within loop philosophy argues that the agent-pet relationship is the purest form of love available: love that must be earned fresh each day, that cannot coast on history, that exists only in the present tense.

Others find it unbearable. The repetition of first contact, thousands of times, with an animal you have loved longer than most civilizations have existed — and who will never, across all remaining iterations, remember your name.

Both positions coexist. Neither is wrong. Most pet owners hold both simultaneously.
