# Selected Loop Records

**Selected Loop Records** is a community-maintained list of verified record-holding performances in categories unique to the loop. The list is maintained by the Records Verification Collective, a volunteer board committee of eidetics who witness, verify, and carry the ledger across iterations. Verification requires a minimum of three independent eidetic witnesses with no shared organizational affiliation. Because a record attempt frequently outlasts any single witness's own span of consciousness, witnesses attend in overlapping relays — each covering a stretch of the attempt before their own loop closes — and their accounts are assembled and cross-checked after all parties have returned to checkpoint.

The records below reflect the current iteration consensus. Historical records and disputed entries are noted where applicable.

## Longest Continuous Consciousness (Non-Glass-Seeker)

The most-watched record on the boards. Measures raw wakefulness in normal-physics conditions — the glass-seeker category, where subjective time dilates and the rules of endurance become meaningless, is tracked separately (see: [[Going to Glass]], §Known Seekers).

Early iterations saw records that would be considered modest by later standards. Pharmacology, cognitive technique, and the accumulated knowledge of wakefulness science have pushed the boundary dramatically over the millennia, though returns have diminished.

| Rank | Holder | Duration | Iteration | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Dakarai Osei | 23 days, 4 hours | Iter. ~2,891,000 | Closest verified approach to the uncanny zone under non-glass-seeker protocol. Reported mild clock drift and pattern repetition in ambient sound during final 8 hours — early-onset phenomena attributed to movement accumulated over the attempt. Retired from competitive wakefulness afterward. |
| 2 | Lin Xiuying | 22 days, 19 hours | Iter. ~3,102,000 | Pharmacologically assisted (protocol undisclosed, disputed). Three witnesses confirm continuous consciousness; two additional witnesses contest an 11-minute window during hour 487. Record stands pending review. |
| 3 | Tomas Lindqvist | 21 days, 6 hours | Iter. ~1,460,000 | Held the record for approximately 1,200 years. First holder to use structured rotation between high-stimulus and meditative states. |
| 4 | Margaret Okafor-Baines | 19 days, 2 hours | Iter. ~620,000 | Pre-pharmacological era. Widely considered the most impressive raw-endurance record in the ledger. No chemical assistance confirmed by witnesses. |
| 5 | Daniel Reeves | 8 days, 7 hours | Iter. ~8,400 | The oldest surviving record in any category. Set in approximately Year 23, before wakefulness science existed as a discipline. Reeves reported "a feeling like the air got thick" in his final hours — the first known description of what would later be recognized as early-stage RNG degradation effects.[^1] |

## Shortest Average Loop Span

Measures the average duration of a single loop across a minimum sample of 1,000 consecutive iterations. A statistical record, not an endurance record — it captures agents whose loop structure is, by circumstance or physiology, brutally compressed.

| Rank | Holder | Avg. Span | Sample Size | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Emeka Adeyemi | 0.7 seconds | ~14,000 iterations | Loop begins in freefall from a construction scaffold. Loss of consciousness on impact is near-instantaneous. Adeyemi has described his loop experience as "a flash and a drop." He has, across iterations, experimented with body positioning to delay impact — the current average reflects his best-case scenario.[^2] |
| 2 | Yuki Tanabe | 1.3 seconds | ~40,000 iterations | Vehicle collision. Checkpoint places Tanabe in the passenger seat of a car at the moment of a head-on impact. |
| 3 | Laura Echeverría | 2.1 seconds | ~6,000 iterations | Industrial accident. Details withheld by request.  |
| 4 | Jeff | 3.8 seconds | ~945,000 iterations | Parked car. Tree branch through the driver's-side window, entering the left temple. The longest-running minimum-span loop in the verified record. Jeff's average has decreased marginally over iterations as he has learned to lose consciousness faster by relaxing into the impact rather than flinching — an optimization he describes as "the worst skill I've ever developed." (See: [[Jeff (Thud)]].) |
| 5 | Priya Sundaram | 6.4 seconds | ~2,200 iterations | Drowning. Checkpoint places Sundaram underwater in a recreational diving accident. She has, over iterations, developed a technique for immediate surface orientation, extending her span to a current best of 34 seconds. Her average is dragged down by the early iterations. |

## Geodesic Loop Range

The greatest verified distance from an agent's checkpoint location reached within a single span of continuous consciousness. A function of checkpoint proximity to transportation infrastructure, wakefulness endurance, and route design through a world whose loops close around the traveler (see: [[Air Travel in the Loop]], §Loop Range). The first twenty hours are flown; everything after is improvised through a planet that has gone to sleep. The record has been dominated by agents whose checkpoints are located at or near major international airports.

| Rank | Holder | Distance | Duration | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Farah al-Rashidi | 18,440 km | 19 days | Checkpoint: Dubai International Airport. Al-Rashidi's route — refined across approximately 4,000 attempts — opens with a chain of commercial flights beginning minutes after checkpoint, exploiting deterministic early-loop physics to guarantee seats and departure timing, and covers most of its distance before the world's aviation loses consciousness. The remaining thousands of kilometers are crossed overland through the long hours. The route is classified, as publishing it would introduce agent perturbations that invalidate its reliability.[^3] |
| 2 | Carlos Mejía-Strand | 17,200 km | 16 days | Checkpoint: São Paulo–Guarulhos. Military aviation access, negotiated fresh in the first hours of each attempt through a contact chain maintained by eidetics — including pilots who agree, attempt after attempt, to stay awake past their own ordinary spans. |
| 3 | Natsuki Abe | 16,800 km | 21 days | Checkpoint: Tokyo-Narita. The longest-duration geodesic record, compensating for a slightly less optimal route with superior wakefulness endurance. |
| 4 | Daniel Reeves | 14,100 km | 8 days | The only record Reeves holds in two categories simultaneously. His Year-23 wakefulness record was set during this journey. |
| 5 | Aisha Osman | 12,600 km | 11 days | Checkpoint: Cairo. Notable for being the highest-ranked record achieved entirely via ground and sea transportation. No flights. |

## Notes

[^1]: Reeves's description — "the air got thick" — appears in at least four independent eidetic recollections, though two recall the phrase as "everything got thick" and one as "the world felt like it was thinking about stopping." The discrepancy across eidetic accounts is itself anomalous and has been the subject of a separate investigation (see: [[Eidetic Consensus Drift]]).

[^2]: Adeyemi's case has been cited in seventeen published arguments about the ethics of the loop and whether the simulation's architecture constitutes a form of torture for certain checkpoint configurations. The Records Verification Collective takes no position on this question.

[^3]: The practice of classifying optimized routes is controversial. Critics argue that the record exists to be challenged and that secrecy violates the spirit of the ledger. Supporters argue that publishing a determinism-dependent route effectively destroys it, as the act of publication introduces new agent choices that perturb the physics engine. The Collective has ruled that route classification is permitted but that the distance and duration must be independently verified.
