# The RNG Degradation

**The RNG Degradation** (also: the Degradation, the Gradient, colloquially "going fuzzy") is the progressive breakdown of quantum randomness as a function of spacetime distance from the reset point. First documented in the Leipzig Conference (~Year 500), it is the most significant scientific discovery in post-loop history and the empirical foundation for Project Dandelion.

The discovery established three things that the Lo-Fi Simulation Theory (see: [[The Lo-Fi Simulation Theory]]) had missed:

1. Quantum randomness is not a feature of the physics engine. It is the output of a random number generator — an algorithm.
2. That algorithm is losing entropy. The RNG's output is degrading, becoming more deterministic over spacetime distance from a fixed point.
3. The fixed point is the checkpoint: October 23, 2011, at the checkpoint moment — an arbitrary instant of UTC time, famously not a round number — at the spatial coordinates of Earth at that moment.

## The Leipzig Moment

The RNG Degradation was formally identified at the Leipzig Conference of approximately Year 500, though anecdotal reports of "anomalous zones" predate the conference by centuries. The breakthrough was the recognition that scattered, individually dismissable observations — coins landing in patterns, Geiger counters ticking in rhythm, shuffled decks producing identical sequences — were not isolated curiosities but expressions of a single underlying process.

The critical insight came from treating these anomalies not as violations of physics but as *measurements* of a quantity: the remaining entropy available to the quantum random number generator. When plotted against spacetime distance from the checkpoint, the anomalies fell on a curve. The curve was smooth. The curve was monotonic. The curve was the same everywhere.

This was not a local phenomenon. It was a global property of the simulation. The RNG was not broken *here and there*. It was broken *everywhere*, in exact proportion to how far *everywhere* was from the reset point.[^1]

## The Spacetime Distance Function

The degradation is not a function of time alone. It is a function of spacetime distance — a four-dimensional measure that incorporates both temporal duration and spatial displacement from the reset point.

This means that agents who stay still experience the degradation more slowly than agents who move. An agent sitting in a room will notice anomalies after roughly 29 days of continuous observation. An agent driving at highway speed will notice them sooner. An agent in low Earth orbit — covering enormous spatial distance while the clock advances — will encounter the degradation threshold in approximately four days.

The counterintuitive result: the astronaut and the long-distance driver hit the same boundary. The astronaut at day 4 and the driver at day 18 are at the same spacetime distance from the checkpoint, measured along different worldlines. The degradation does not care how fast you are going. It cares how far you are. Speed simply covers more distance.

| Agent Condition | Approximate Observable Threshold |
|---|---|
| Stationary observer | ~29 days |
| Ground vehicle (~100 km/h) | ~15-20 days |
| Commercial aircraft (~900 km/h) | ~7-10 days |
| Low Earth orbit (~27,500 km/h) | ~4 days |

These figures represent the point at which the degradation becomes measurable with standard instruments. Subtle statistical anomalies begin well before the observable threshold.

Notably, an ordinary loop never approaches the threshold. A typical agent's span of consciousness ends within twenty hours of checkpoint, and even the most widely-traveled agent covers negligible spacetime distance in that window. The degradation is encountered only by agents who sustain wakefulness far beyond the ordinary span — and sooner by those who cover distance while doing it.

## Phenomenology

The degradation manifests as a progressive loss of quantum randomness. The RNG's output becomes increasingly deterministic — not in the sense that physics breaks, but in the sense that statistically independent events begin to correlate.

**What the degradation IS:** Statistical anomalies that are individually dismissable and collectively impossible:

- Coins producing the same sequence on every toss
- Geiger counter clicks resolving into periodic rhythms
- Identical cloud formations appearing over different continents
- Shuffled decks producing identical orderings
- Radio static resolving into tonal patterns
- Unrelated strangers saying the same sentence at the same moment
- Repeated experiments producing identical results where variance is expected

The world begins to *rhyme with itself*. Events that should be independent start echoing. Patterns appear in noise. The noise sounds less and less like noise.

**What the degradation is NOT:** Conservation laws are hard-coded assertions in the physics engine (see: [[The Lo-Fi Simulation Theory]], §Conservation Laws as Assertions). They are enforced on every tick regardless of the RNG's state. The degradation therefore does NOT produce:

- Violations of energy or momentum conservation
- Spontaneous creation or destruction of matter
- Teleportation or spatial discontinuities
- Changes to the strong or weak forces

The distinction is structural: the RNG handles probability and quantum selection. The assertions handle accounting. The degradation corrupts `rand()`. It does not corrupt `assert()`. This is why the degradation is so uncanny — physics still *works*. Every individual event is physically legal. It is only the *distribution* of events that becomes impossible.

## Progressive Zones

As spacetime distance from the checkpoint increases, the degradation intensifies through recognized stages:

1. **The Subtle Zone.** Statistical improbabilities detectable only with instruments or obsessive record-keeping. Coin sequences deviate from expected distributions. Radioactive decay rates become suspiciously regular. Most agents never notice. The ones who do spend years doubting themselves. This is the zone that drove people to the Leipzig Conference — not because it was dramatic, but because it was *almost not there*.

2. **The Uncanny Zone.** Macro-scale pattern formation. Clock drift across independent timepieces. Sounds dropping in pitch (audio waveforms losing frequency variance). Sunsets lasting too long (atmospheric scattering calculations converging). Trees standing unnaturally still (air currents losing turbulent variation). The world looks right. The world feels wrong. The uncanny zone is where most glass-seekers turn back.

3. **The Dilation Zone.** Physics begins running measurably slower relative to consciousness. The agent's thoughts outpace the world. Movement becomes sluggish, then glacial. This is not time dilation in the relativistic sense — the physics engine is running normally on its own clock. It is the *agent engine* that remains at full speed while the physics engine, starved of entropy, slows its resolution of probabilistic outcomes. The agent experiences this as the world thickening, hardening, slowing to a crawl.

4. **Vitrification.** The terminal state. The world has effectively stopped. The RNG output has fully exhausted its structure and collapsed to deterministic repetition. The probability space has coalesced and crystallized in spacetime — no remaining entropy to resolve, no remaining outcomes to compute. Physics becomes a lattice. Consciousness moves through it like light through glass. (See: [[The Boundary / Vitrification (Going to Glass)]] for the full phenomenological account.)

## The Key Question

The article, as of this writing, can describe the degradation with precision. It cannot explain it. The open questions include:

- **Why is the entropy declining?** The RNG should be drawing from a functionally infinite source. What is depleting it?
- **Why is the fixed point the checkpoint?** The degradation radiates outward from the checkpoint moment as if the checkpoint *is* the entropy source. This would mean the checkpoint is not merely a temporal bookmark but an active computational event — something that *generates* the entropy the physics engine consumes.
- **Is the degradation cumulative across iterations?** Measurements suggest it resets each loop along with everything else physical. But if it resets, why does the RNG output differ on the same worldline across different iterations? (This question is unresolved and forms the basis of several active research programs.)
- **Can the degradation be reversed, halted, or exploited?** This is Project Dandelion's founding question. See: [[Project Dandelion]].

## Relationship to Lo-Fi Theory

The RNG Degradation is simultaneously the greatest vindication of and the most devastating challenge to the Lo-Fi Simulation Theory. The Theory predicted a computational substrate. The Degradation *proved* it — not through analogy, but through measurement. For the first time, an aspect of the simulation could be observed *malfunctioning*, producing output that was measurably wrong against its own specifications.

But the Theory also missed the RNG entirely. Every correspondence in the Lo-Fi framework treats quantum randomness as a feature — floating-point imprecision, lazy rendering, probabilistic optimization. The Degradation revealed that randomness is not a design feature. It is the output of a component. That component is failing.

The Theory provided the vocabulary. The Degradation gave it something to say.[^2]
