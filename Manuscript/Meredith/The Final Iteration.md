October 23rd, Roanoke, Virginia
3,650,753
Meredith

She saw it long before she felt it.

It came the way heat comes off a desert road on some summer-vacation drive — looking down the long flat stretch ahead of you, the shimmer rising off the blacktop, warming the air just enough to tell you that you are seeing something that is not quite there, and not knowing which part of what you see to trust. It started as a pinpoint. Then it spread as a circle. Then it was a wave across her whole field of vision, and the wave seemed to be made of the light itself — light that had stopped obeying, light that let her see around corners, and before long, let her see the back of her own head.

The light coiled around her, tighter and tighter, a vise made of seeing. Things that were far away came toward her — not moving, just *arriving* — drawn closer and closer to her vision until they pressed flat and stacked, everything she could see in those last transpiring moments and everything anyone could possibly have seen, from any angle, anywhere near her, all at the same time, sandwiched into a stratified lasagna of the visible world.

And as the vise tightened on her eyes, something happened to the substance reality was made of. Not the air going heavy — not some hundred-percent Deep-South humidity, not even the viscosity of a swimming pool. It was more like something invisible and clear enveloping her and beginning to harden, as if resin had been poured over everything and was starting to set. The vibration going out of her atoms. The will going out of her arms. The twitch going out of her eye. Slowly: tighter. Tighter. Tighter. A straitjacket compressing every centimeter of her skin, down to her lashes, down to her corneas, locking her into place the way a gold medalist's grip ends a jiu-jitsu match — slower, and slower, and slower.

Darkness falling, but consciousness staying. The ability to move, gone. The ability to shift her gaze, gone. The ability to sleep — the one door that had always, always opened — gone. What remained was pure observation of nothing in particular. No movement, anywhere. A physics engine ground to a halt.

Meredith had heard the term *going to glass* before.

Now she knew what it meant.

If spacetime had given her the ability, in this moment, she would shudder.
