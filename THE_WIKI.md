## The In-Universe Encyclopedia — Content Map and Editorial Culture
### How hard physics reaches the reader through a document rebuilt from memory every day

> This document maps the wiki as a **narrative vehicle**: which articles exist, what they cover,
> how the editorial culture works, and where in the novel we dip into wiki-voice.
> Cross-reference with THE_LIBRARY.md for theory correctness and THE_BIBLE.md for ground truth.

## HOW THE WIKI WORKS

### The Mechanism

The wiki is the loop's version of Wikipedia — a collaboratively written, encyclopedic knowledge base. The catch: it exists on the internet, which resets every iteration. There is no midnight and no shared clock (THE_BIBLE.md §3: the checkpoint is an arbitrary UTC instant; resets are per-agent, on loss of consciousness) — but human biology gives the loop a quasi-daily rhythm: nearly everyone's span opens at checkpoint and closes 16–20 hours later. The scribes — eidetics and fast typists working in relay teams — start rebuilding at first light (C+0, the moment the checkpoint restores) and race the waking of the checkpoint sleepers. Within a few hours of checkpoint, the core articles are live.

This means:

- **The content drifts.** A scribe misremembers a sentence. Another scribe "corrects" it with their own misremembering. Over centuries, articles evolve not through deliberate revision but through the accumulated noise of human memory under duress.
- **The content is contested in real-time.** Every iteration is a fresh editorial battleground. An article that was stable for a thousand iterations can get hijacked in a single first-light window if the right (or wrong) scribe gets to a terminal first.
- **There is no version history.** No diff. No talk page that survives the reset. Editorial disputes are carried in human memory, which means grudges are permanent but evidence is not.
- **The articles are the closest thing to institutional memory the loop has.** The boards are chaotic. The wiki is *curated* chaos.

### The Eidetics

The wiki can't exist without the eidetics, and the eidetics can't exist without the wiki. They're the celebrities of loop society — walking hard drives in a world where nothing else persists. 2,451 identified, 487 polymaths, 14 polyglots, 4 polymath-polyglots. Everyone knows the numbers. Everyone has opinions about the people behind them.

They're an odd bunch. Perfect memory across thousands of iterations does things to a person. Some are meticulous archivists who treat recall as sacred duty. Some are theatrical performers who exploit their rarity for status. Some are deeply unreliable narrators who remember everything perfectly and lie about it anyway — because they can, because who's going to check? Some are all three depending on the day.

The thing about eidetics is: they must maintain their reputations *across millennia*. A normal person can reinvent themselves every iteration. An eidetic is known. Their history is their brand. Get caught fabricating a memory once, and that follows you for ten thousand years. This creates a strange economy of trust — eidetics are simultaneously the most reliable and most dangerous sources of information, because the ones who *choose* to lie are terrifyingly good at it.

Caius sits at the crossroads. Polymath-polyglot. Occasional wiki contributor. Political operator. He doesn't maintain any article, but he's edited most of them. His relationship with the wiki is the same as his relationship with everything: he uses it, shapes it, benefits from it, and never fully commits to it. The scribes tolerate him because his edits are *usually* accurate and *always* strategic. They don't trust him because nobody trusts Caius.

### The Scribes

Not a formal organization — more like a guild culture. Self-selected eidetics who take pride in accurate reconstruction. Some specialize: one person owns the Lo-Fi Theory article, another owns the Going to Glass phenomenology article, another maintains the timeline. "Ownership" is informal and fiercely defended.

There's status in being a scribe. There's also resentment — non-eidetics sometimes view the scribes as gatekeepers of knowledge, deciding what gets remembered "correctly" and what slowly drifts out of the record. The scribes see themselves as custodians. Their critics see them as priests.

### The Editorial Wars

This is where the comedy lives. The wiki is rebuilt from memory, which means:

- **Operative edits.** People with agendas drill their checkpoint-to-terminal time and write their version first. By the time the "real" scribe reaches a keyboard — slowed by a checkpoint body that wakes across town, or by being a checkpoint sleeper whose loop starts hours late — the article has been live for hours with subtle distortions. Correcting it looks like the edit war, not the original sabotage.
- **Personal beefs.** A scribe who got publicly embarrassed changes a rival's contribution just enough to be wrong. Not enough to be obvious. The kind of petty that only immortals with perfect memory and infinite time can sustain.
- **Philosophical schisms expressed through comma placement.** One faction insists the Lo-Fi article should say the speed of light "is" the render distance. Another insists it should say "functions as" the render distance. This distinction has consumed more human hours than Project Dandelion.
- **Humor as weapon.** Someone adds a joke footnote. Someone else removes it. Someone else adds it back attributed to a different person. The footnote becomes more famous than the article.
- **Deliberately absurd entries.** The wiki equivalent of Wikipedia vandalism, except the vandal has had centuries to perfect the art. Articles that are 99% accurate with one sentence of beautifully crafted nonsense buried in the middle.

### Core Physics Articles

#### 1. The Lo-Fi Simulation Theory **[EXCERPT — FULL ARTICLE]**

**Coverage:** The flagship article. The one everyone's read. Argues that physical constants are computational constraints:
- Speed of light as render distance / draw distance
- Planck length as pixel size / resolution limit
- Heisenberg uncertainty as floating-point precision limit
- Quantum energy levels as computational simplification
- Wave-particle duality as lazy probabilistic rendering (only resolves when observed)
- Pauli Exclusion Principle as data-density limit
- Conservation laws as hard-coded assertions (`assert()`, not derivations)
- Observer effect as lazy evaluation / render-on-demand

**When it appears:** Era 2 onward. Begins as a fringe article, gains mainstream acceptance by Era 3. By Era 4 it's the baseline intellectual framework — not because it's proven, but because it's *useful*. Every subsequent discovery is discussed in its vocabulary.

**What it gets right:** Substantially correct about the *what*. The universe IS a simulation. Physical constants ARE implementation constraints.

**What it gets wrong:** Misses the RNG entirely. Treats quantum randomness as a feature (floating-point imprecision) rather than what it actually is (a random number generator with a degrading entropy source). This is the article's fatal blind spot — and its greatest contribution is making the RNG discovery *thinkable* once someone looks at randomness differently.

**What it doesn't cover:** The dual-engine architecture. The article treats consciousness as an emergent property of the physics engine, not as a separate system. This is wrong, but nobody knows that until much later.

**Editorial drama:**
- The "is" vs "functions as" war (render distance framing)
- A faction that insists on including a "Criticisms" section vs. a faction that considers criticism of the theory to be bad-faith denialism
- Someone keeps adding "see also: God" as a redirect. It keeps getting removed. It keeps coming back.
- The article's prose style has drifted from academic to almost literary over millennia, because the scribes who maintain it are also the people most in love with it

**Existing manuscript material:** The Caius/Anaya dialogue in `Manuscript/CERN/Physical Limits.rtf` covers the same arguments in conversation form. The wiki article is the encyclopedic version of what Anaya explains verbally.

**Manuscript:** `Manuscript/Interstitials/Wiki/The Lo-Fi Simulation Theory.md`

#### 2. The Reset **[EXCERPT]**

**Coverage:** What happens at the checkpoint moment (an arbitrary UTC instant — famously not a round number), what resets (everything physical), what doesn't (memory, consciousness, learned knowledge). The per-agent trigger: loss of consciousness ends your loop, whenever it comes. The jump cut. The checkpoint sleepers — the fraction of humanity whose checkpoint bodies were asleep and whose loops begin when those bodies wake. The unobservability of the rollback itself (you are always on the wrong side of a reset).

**When it appears:** Era 1 onward — this is one of the first articles written. Possibly THE first.

**What it gets right:** The observational facts. The consciousness-loss trigger. Physical state restoration. Memory persistence.

**What it gets wrong (early):** Early versions try to explain memory persistence through neurological mechanisms (the wiki equivalent of WORLD_RULES.md's deprecated quantum neurogenesis). These explanations are progressively abandoned as they fail to hold up, replaced by "mechanism unknown, phenomenon confirmed" language.

**What it doesn't cover:** The dual-engine architecture (until very late, if ever). The engine mismatch phenomena (accumulating mental anomalies over iterations) — these are observed and tracked in separate medical articles but not connected to the reset mechanism.

**Editorial drama:**
- The "consciousness persists" line is politically charged. Some people don't WANT it explained — it feels sacred. Others consider the refusal to investigate it anti-scientific. This is a genuine philosophical divide, not a petty squabble.
- A perennial debate about whether to include the "subjective experience" section (what reset feels like from inside) or keep the article purely observational.

#### 3. The Boundary / Vitrification (Going to Glass)

**Coverage:** The spacetime boundary where physics degrades and eventually stops. The ~29-day threshold for stationary observers. The velocity-dependence (ISS astronauts hit it faster). The phenomenology of vitrification — the dilation, the dissolution, the conviction. Colloquially known as "going to glass" for millennia before the scientific term catches on.

**When it appears:** Era 5 onward. Before Era 5, the boundary is rumor and myth. The article codifies it.

**What it gets right:** The observational phenomenology is accurate. The spacetime-distance dependence is correctly identified once the data comes in.

**What it gets wrong:** Early versions frame it as "reaching the edge of the simulation" — a wall you hit. The RNG degradation model (it's not an edge, it's a gradient of entropy exhaustion) comes later and is hard to accept because the "wall" metaphor is more intuitive.

**What it doesn't cover:** The subjective experience in detail — that's considered too personal / variable to encyclopedize. The article notes that "accounts vary significantly" and defers to individual testimony. This is the wiki's way of acknowledging that going to glass is individual, not universal.

**Editorial drama:**
- Glass-seekers keep adding spiritual/mystical language. The scientific scribes keep removing it. The article oscillates between "physics phenomenon" and "transcendent experience" depending on who got to the terminal first.
- A running argument about whether "going to glass" or "vitrification" or "reaching the boundary" is the correct neutral terminology. "Going to glass" wins by cultural momentum, not editorial consensus.

#### 4. The RNG Degradation **[EXCERPT — FULL ARTICLE]**

**Coverage:** The discovery that quantum outcomes are deterministic. The Leipzig Moment. The spacetime-distance function. The realization that the "randomness" of quantum mechanics is generated by an algorithm, and that algorithm is losing entropy from a fixed point.

**When it appears:** Era 4 (Leipzig Moment) onward. Undergoes massive expansion during Eras 5-6 as the implications sink in.

**What it gets right:** Nearly everything, eventually. This is the article that gets closest to ground truth, because the RNG is the thing the agents can actually measure.

**What it gets wrong:** The article can describe WHAT is happening but not WHY. It doesn't know about the checkpoint disrupting the entropy source. It doesn't know about the CI/CD suite. It can map the degradation curve without knowing the cause.

**What it doesn't cover:** The feedback loop between RNG prediction and glass (i.e., the timequake mechanism). Nobody theorizes this before it happens.

**Editorial drama:**
- This article is the most technically dense and the least accessible. There's a permanent tension between the specialists who want mathematical rigor and the populists who want "explain it like I'm 500 years old"
- The gentleman scientists who made the discovery resist having their work summarized by scribes who don't fully understand it. But they also can't type fast enough to write the article themselves before the day ends.

**Manuscript:** `Manuscript/Interstitials/Wiki/The RNG Degradation.md`

#### 5. Project Dandelion

**Coverage:** The civilization-scale effort to reverse-engineer the RNG. The computational campaign. The human infrastructure required. The political negotiations that made it possible.

**When it appears:** Era 6 onward.

**What it gets right:** The operational facts. What the project does, how it's organized, what resources it requires.

**What it gets wrong:** Nothing factual — but the article's *framing* evolves. Early versions are triumphalist ("humanity's greatest collective achievement"). Late versions, post-timequake, don't exist — there's nobody left to write them.

**Editorial drama:**
- The most politically contentious article in the wiki. Dandelion required a global vote and centuries of cooperation. The article is a proxy war for people who supported vs. opposed the project.
- Caius's role is either heroic or dictatorial depending on who's writing.

### Medical & Phenomenological Articles

#### 10. Engine Mismatch Phenomena (unnamed in-universe)

**Coverage:** The accumulating mental anomalies that increase with iteration count. Novel experiences that don't map to any known neurological or psychological condition. In-universe, this article probably has a clinical name — something like "Iteration-Dependent Cognitive Phenomena" or more colloquially "the drift."

**When it appears:** Era 3-4 onward. Slow to gain traction because the effects are subtle early.

**What it gets right:** The observational data. The correlation with iteration count.

**What it gets wrong:** No mechanism. The article catalogs without explaining, because the explanation (engine mismatch between agent and physics substrates) requires knowledge nobody has.

**Editorial drama:**
- A faction that wants to fold this into the Reset article. A faction that insists it's distinct.
- People who experience severe drift symptoms feel the article trivializes their condition. People who don't experience them think it's hypochondria.

### Prediction Markets & Social Infrastructure

#### 11. WTFreality **[EXCERPT — FULL ARTICLE]**

**Coverage:** The in-universe prediction market — the loop's version of Polymarket. A constantly-updating betting site where people wager on when and how the loop breaks. Not for money (money resets). For reputation. For being the person who called it.

**Structure:** A leaderboard of the top 5 leading theories, ranked by betting odds (rebuilt from memorized state every iteration). Updated through the shared hours as events unfold. Categories include:

- **Will the loop break today?** (Perpetual market. Has never paid out. The "no" position has a 10,000-year win streak.)
- **If it breaks, what caused it?** (Voyager correction: 12:1. RNG exhaustion: 8:1. Divine intervention: 45:1. Jeff's driving somehow: 200:1. Caius did something: 6:1.)
- **How far after checkpoint?** (Side markets on checkpoint offsets — a break deep in the long hours would be witnessed by almost no one, and a missing reset is the last thing anyone would notice.)
- **Will we remember?** (The existential market. If the loop breaks, do we keep our memories? 3:1 yes. 2:1 no. 5:1 "depends on what 'we' means.")

**When it appears:** Era 2 onward. Starts as informal bets on the boards, formalizes into a standing institution.

**What makes it interesting:**
- The odds are a real-time sentiment tracker of collective theory belief. When the Leipzig Moment hits, the "simulation-related cause" category's odds collapse within a single iteration.
- The WTFreality leaderboard is rebuilt from memory every iteration, which means the odds themselves are subject to scribe drift. Someone who's been running WTFreality for 500 years has enormous influence over "what the market thinks."
- Author-level irony (NOT in the standing article, which must predate the timequake to exist in-universe): the market has the correct cause ("RNG-related event") at 8:1 — pretty good for a prediction market, completely useless because nobody bet on "the RNG team succeeds and it destroys everything." If a final-act wiki excerpt ever exists, this is its payload. The standing article instead carries the pre-test note: the Oracles, almost unanimously, declined to price the live test.

**Editorial drama:**
- Is WTFreality a wiki article or a board feature? Both communities claim it. The wiki article *about* WTFreality is separate from WTFreality itself.
- The odds are a political weapon. Lowering a theory's odds is a way to delegitimize it without arguing the science.

**Manuscript:** `Manuscript/Interstitials/Wiki/WTFreality.md`

#### 18. Satoshi Furukawa **[EXCERPT candidate]**

**Coverage:** Biography of the loop's best-known orbital agent. JAXA flight engineer and physician (surgeon for a decade before astronaut selection), aboard the ISS at checkpoint — Expedition 29, with Mike Fossum and Sergey Volkov, the station's real crew on October 23, 2011. The self-experiment program: wakefulness runs toward the day-4 boundary, clinical self-observation with diagnostic rigor, logs memorized and radioed to ground eidetics. His centuries of dismissed anomaly reports, later recognized as the earliest systematic documentation of the degradation. The Soyuz question: he can be on Earth in under four hours, and mostly, he isn't.

**When it appears:** Era 2 onward as a curiosity ("the men who live in the sky"); rewritten substantially post-Leipzig, when his old logs are reread as data.

**What it gets right:** The observational record — his logs are carried by ground eidetics with unusual fidelity, because radio relay forces them to be dictated in finished sentences.

**What it gets wrong:** Early versions frame him as a mystic ("the man who watches the world stop"). He has requested corrections. From orbit, enforcement is difficult.

**Editorial drama:**
- The article is maintained by a scribe who has never met him and never can — the entire relationship is radio, relay, and edits. Both parties have described this as "a normal working relationship, by loop standards."
- A recurring dispute over whether Fossum and Volkov warrant their own articles or sections within his. Current consensus: sections. The talk page calls this "the crew problem."

**Cross-references:** THE_BIBLE.md §6 ("Furukawa as Orbital Experimentalist") and §7 Era 5 (the orbital crew's boundary discovery). His experiments are the orbital branch of [[Neuroscience in the Loop]]'s interface program and the empirical anchor for the velocity rows in [[The RNG Degradation]]'s threshold table.

## RECURRING ARTICLES — The Six That Evolve

Not all wiki articles are static reference material. Approximately six articles are revisited across different iterations throughout the novel, showing how they evolve as understanding deepens. These are the "show don't tell" backbone — the reader watches the wiki's understanding change without anyone explaining that understanding has changed.

### How This Works

Each of these articles appears in at least two widely-separated iterations. The differences between versions reveal what the characters have learned, what they've abandoned, and what they still get wrong. The reader doesn't need a narrator to say "by Era 4, scientists had discovered X" — the reader reads Version 1 of an article, then reads Version 2 a hundred pages later, and the delta *is* the narrative.

### The Six

| Article | First Version (approx.) | Later Version(s) | What Changes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Lo-Fi Simulation Theory** | Era 2 (speculative, excited, "what if this is all a game?") | Era 4+ (settled, foundational, taken for granted) | Tone shifts from revolutionary to textbook. The "Criticisms" section shrinks. Someone has added a "Historical Context" section. |
| **The Reset** | Era 1 (desperate, observational, "here is what happens to us") | Era 3+ (clinical, comprehensive, spirituality debate visible in structure) | Early version reads like a survivor's account. Late version reads like a medical textbook with a controversial appendix. |
| **The RNG Degradation** | Era 4 (brand new, contested, "a retired chemistry teacher claims...") | Era 6+ (authoritative, dense, mathematical, "it is well established that...") | From fringe discovery to settled science. The gentleman-scientist origin story gets its own subsection. |
| **The Boundary / Going to Glass** | Era 5 (mythological, experiential, "reports suggest that if you stay awake long enough...") | Era 6+ (scientific, connected to RNG degradation, "vitrification is a consequence of...") | From campfire story to unified theory. The otter gets its own paragraph. Someone adds a "Safety Warnings" section that is both grim and funny. |
| **Bug 1 Theories** | Era 2 (long, catholic, includes God and aliens as serious entries) | Era 6+ (shorter, more curated, Voyager theory prominent, "the following theories have been largely abandoned...") | The list shrinks. Tone shifts from "here are our guesses" to "here is what we've eliminated." Religious theories get a "historical" designation that infuriates believers. |
| **Caius Middleton** | Era 3 (brief, political focus, "architect of the CERN coalition") | Era 6+ (enormous, contested, "political leader" vs "political operator" war visible in talk-page-equivalent disputes) | From one-paragraph biographical stub to the most edit-warred article in the wiki. The energy-spike failure section grows. The Dandelion section is begrudging. |

### Why This Matters

The recurring articles give the novel a structural trick that's hard to pull off in traditional prose: **unreliable institutional memory evolving in real time**. The wiki doesn't have a narrator. It doesn't have an opinion. It has an aggregate belief state that shifts, and the reader watches it shift from the inside. This is how the reader learns that the characters are getting smarter — not because someone says so, but because the reference material has changed.

### What reaches the reader through the wiki

| Physics Concept | Wiki Article | How the Reader Encounters It |
|---|---|---|
| Simulation architecture (constants as constraints) | Lo-Fi Simulation Theory | Wiki excerpt — the flagship piece |

### What DOESN'T reach the reader through the wiki

| Physics Concept | Delivery Channel | Why Not Wiki |
|---|---|---|
| Going to glass (experience) | Character narration | Too subjective, too individual. The wiki article on the boundary covers the physics; the experience is lived, not documented. |
| Engine mismatch (felt) | Character narration | The symptoms are felt before they're cataloged. Characters experience the drift before the article exists. |
| The timequake | Lived event / Australian news broadcast | There is no wiki after the timequake. The scribes are in glass. |
| Dual-engine architecture | Postscript / reader inference | Nobody in-universe ever fully names this. The wiki approaches it sideways through the Reset article and the mismatch article, but never connects the dots. |
| CI/CD suite / automated fix | Postscript only | Completely unknown to agents. Not even theorized. |
| Bug 1 cause (ground truth) | Deliberately never delivered | Syrah's theory is the closest, presented as one of many. The novel doesn't confirm it. |

## WIKI MOMENTS IN THE NOVEL

Planned or potential scenes where the wiki is foregrounded — either through excerpt, reference, or editorial drama:

### 1. First Encounter (Early Era)
A character discovers the wiki for the first time. Reads the Lo-Fi Theory article. The reader gets the physics through fresh eyes — someone encountering these ideas and finding them either brilliant or insane.

### 2. The Caius/Anaya Scene (Era 3, existing manuscript)
Already written in `Manuscript/CERN/Physical Limits.rtf`. This is the *dialogue version* of the Lo-Fi article's content. The wiki article post-dates this conversation — Anaya's ideas become wiki canon.

### 3. An Editorial War (comedic interstitial)
A loop in the life of a scribe. Snaps back to checkpoint, races to a terminal, discovers someone has already rewritten their article with subtle errors. The ensuing correction spiral. Could be played for comedy with real human stakes underneath — this person's identity is "the person who maintains Article X," and someone is trying to take that from them.

### 4. The Leipzig Moment Wiki Update
The iteration after the beam splitter discovery. A new scribe tries to add the results to the RNG article. The existing maintainer resists — the data contradicts the Lo-Fi Theory's treatment of quantum uncertainty as floating-point error. If outcomes are *deterministic*, not imprecise, the whole framework needs rethinking. The editorial fight mirrors the scientific one.

### 5. Post-Timequake Silence
The last article never gets finished. A scribe was mid-sentence when the wavefront arrived. The next iteration, there is no next iteration. The wiki dies unwritten for the first time in millennia.

## NOTES

- The wiki is NOT the boards. The boards are chaotic, real-time, argumentative. The wiki is curated, encyclopedic, and aspires to neutrality (while constantly failing at it). Think Wikipedia vs. Reddit.
- Articles can be referenced without being excerpted. A character saying "the wiki says X" is a lighter touch than a full encyclopedic passage.
- The editorial drama should be FUNNY. These are immortals with perfect memory having petty arguments about comma placement. The comedy is the contrast between the stakes (understanding the nature of reality) and the execution (two scribes who hate each other fighting over whether to say "is" or "functions as").
- The wiki's greatest blind spot is the dual-engine architecture. It can describe everything *about* consciousness's weird relationship to the reset without ever naming WHY. This is the reader's reward — assembling the answer from the wiki's conspicuous gap.
